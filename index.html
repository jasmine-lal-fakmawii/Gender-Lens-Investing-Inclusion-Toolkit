Marketing & outreach checklist
  • Use images and language that reflect target clients (women, minority groups, persons with disability) in all materials
  • Translate key materials into local languages and dialects
  • Offer radio and WhatsApp outreach, not just print or branch visits
  • Host information sessions at community venues (markets, health centres, schools)
  • Assign at least one female loan officer or relationship manager to women-led pipeline
  • Remove time constraints that disadvantage carers (offer evening / weekend outreach sessions)
  • Offer application support sessions for first-time borrowers with low literacy
  • Partner with trusted community intermediaries for referral
  • Ensure digital applications are mobile-friendly and offline-capable
  • Communicate clearly what data will be collected and how it will be used (data consent)
Common pipeline barriers to fix
These barriers systematically exclude women and marginalised groups from ever entering your pipeline. Address them before marketing.
  • Collateral requirements Women often lack land titles or formal assets. Use moveable assets, guarantees, or group liability instead.
  • Formal registration bias Many women-led SMEs are unregistered. Accept alternate proof: supplier contracts, mobile money history, community references.
  • Credit history gaps Women have shorter or absent credit histories. Use cash flow-based or psychometric credit assessment.
  • Spousal consent requirements These are discriminatory in many contexts. Review local law; default to individual consent wherever possible.
  • Branch / city-centric operations Use mobile officers, agents, and digital channels to reach peri-urban and rural entrepreneurs.

Collateral alternatives (IFC-aligned)
  • Moveable asset financing e.g. Accept equipment, livestock, inventory as collateral where legal frameworks allow
  • Group / solidarity guarantee e.g.Peer group co-guarantees; especially effective in savings group communities
  • Cash flow-based lending e.g.Underwrite against verified mobile money, supplier payment, or market stall receipts
  • Partial credit guarantee schemes e.g.DFI or government-backed guarantees covering 50–80% of loan value
  • Invoice / purchase order financing e.g.Use confirmed supply chain receivables as collateral
  • Digital footprint scoring e.g.Mobile transaction history, e-commerce sales data, utility payment records
Pricing & terms checklist
  • Offer interest rate incentive (0.5–2% reduction) for hitting gender/inclusion milestones (e.g. hiring women managers)
  • Flexible repayment schedules aligned with seasonal or irregular cash flows
  • No hidden fees; present total cost of credit (TCC) clearly
  • Offer loan top-up mechanism after 6–12 months of on-time repayment
  • Include a hardship deferral clause for crisis, illness, or conflict disruption
  • Bundle with savings or insurance product where available (IFC linkage banking)
  • Set equity investment terms with board seat or advisory rights only where governance is genuinely strengthened (not extractive)

Appraisal and selection procedures must be structured to overcome unconscious bias and institutional barriers. This section provides a step-by-step due diligence workflow, interview guide, and red-flag checklist aligned with IFC and GIIN standards.
Due diligence workflow: gender and social inclusion

Assess the SME's core business model using a gender-informed lens. Key questions:

  • What markets does the business serve, and are these accessible to women or excluded groups?
  • Does the business model depend on informal relationships that may not survive credit formalisation?
  • What is the owner's household situation, e.g. caregiving burden, mobility, decision-making autonomy?
  • Are sales seasonal? Map the repayment schedule against identified lean seasons.
  • Has the business survived a prior crisis, shock, or displacement event? What adaptive strategies were used?
IRIS+ indicators: OI7505 (gender of senior leadership), PI4439 (female employees), PI7519 (ownership structure)
  • Reconstruct cash flows from mobile money, market stall sales, or supplier receipts if formal accounts absent
  • Use 12-month trailing analysis; do not penalise seasonal gaps in turnover
  • Adjust DSCR calculations to account for household income that may subsidise the business during lean periods
  • Do not use household asset ownership as a negative credit indicator if assets are held in husband's name due to customary law
  • For group lending: assess collective repayment capacity, not just individual member capacity
Bias check: Are you applying stricter standards to this applicant than you would to a male-led business with equivalent cash flows?
  • Review IFC Performance Standard 2: Labour and working conditions: does the business have adequate worker protections?
  • Screen for child labour, forced labour, or debt bondage (especially in supply chains)
  • Assess gender-based violence (GBV) risk: is the loan likely to increase the applicant's risk of partner violence?
  • Review environmental impact: does the business disproportionately harm women or marginalised communities nearby?
  • Apply GIIN COIN (Communities of Impact) lens for community-level effects
  • For equity investments: map board composition against 2X Leadership criteria (≥30% women on board)
  • Assess whether the nominated borrower has genuine decision-making control over the business
  • Check for nominee structures that mask actual ownership (a male relative listed as co-owner)
  • For larger SMEs: review HR policies for gender pay equity, parental leave, anti-harassment
  • IRIS+ indicator: OI7505, OI5550 (women in senior management)
  • Run a structured bias audit: would this application have been assessed differently if the owner were male?
  • Require sign-off from a second officer if application is declined for a woman-led or minority-led SME
  • Document the specific reason for decline and track these reasons across the portfolio quarterly
  • If declined, offer a transparent feedback session and pathway to reapply (with TA support if needed)
  • Set a minimum portfolio target for gender-lens deals (recommend ≥40% of SME book) and track against it monthly
Red flag: If decline rate for women-led applications is more than 1.5x the rate for male-led applications with equivalent financials, review your appraisal criteria immediately.

Early warning signals
Women and excluded borrowers may signal distress differently. Do not rely solely on missed payments.
  • ⚑
    Client becomes unreachable or changes communication channel abruptly
  • ⚑
    Business operating hours or location changes unexpectedly
  • ⚑
    Third party (male relative) begins answering client's phone
  • ⚑
    Repayment amounts drop below contracted schedule
  • ⚑
    Client requests change of loan account holder name
  • ⚑
    Regional security or climate event in client's area
  • ⚑
    Key supplier or buyer relationship disrupted
At-risk client response protocol
  • Trigger outreach within 5 days of missed payment or warning signal, use preferred channel
  • Offer payment holiday (1–3 months) before escalating to recovery, especially in crisis contexts
  • Do NOT pursue aggressive collection practices with women clients, document and review any complaints
  • Connect at-risk client to TA partner or business advisory service
  • Review loan terms for restructuring before writing off
  • If GBV risk identified, refer to GBV service partner. Do not proceed with standard collection
  • Document outcome in portfolio management system with gender and social tags
Technical assistance (TA) is beyond a "nice to have" for gender-lens portfolios. It is a return-enhancing, risk-reducing investment. This section outlines how to design, source, and deliver TA that genuinely serves women-led and socially excluded SMEs.
TA design principles
Demand-led: Ask clients what they need. Do not impose standardised training curricula. Use a needs assessment at onboarding and at each annual review.
Integrated, not siloed: TA should be embedded in the lending relationship, not handed off to a separate NGO with no coordination. Assign a TA contact to each loan officer.
Context-sensitive: In fragile markets, digital / remote TA is often more effective than in-person. Use WhatsApp groups, voice notes, and peer learning circles.
Gender-transformative: Beyond business skills, address gender norms that limit women's decision-making power, mobility, and control over earnings.

TA onboarding checklist (at loan disbursement)
  • Complete TA needs assessment form with client (not just loan officer's guess)
  • Assign to at least one TA track; introduce to TA provider within 30 days of disbursement
  • Confirm TA delivery is in client's preferred language
  • Set 90-day TA milestone and review at first loan monitoring visit
  • Record TA participation in portfolio management system linked to client file
  • Confirm client knows TA is free and separate from loan repayment obligations

Do no harm — baseline requirement in all crisis contexts
  • Assess whether loan disbursement could increase GBV risk (e.g. if male household members control finances)
  • Disburse to client's own mobile money or individual account, not joint or male-named account
  • Do not share client data with government or security actors without informed consent
  • Establish a GBV referral pathway before portfolio launch in crisis context
  • Apply ICRC humanitarian principles: humanity, neutrality, impartiality

Displacement & refugee context
Key principle (UNHCR / IFC): Refugees and IDPs have the right to financial services. Legal right to work is the key variable. Check national law before building product.
  • Verify legal right to work and operate a business for the target client population
  • Accept UNHCR registration card or national ID equivalent as KYC document
  • Build credit history using camp market receipts, savings group records, or NGO payment history
  • Design for potential movement such as no fixed-location clauses; digital loan management
  • Partner with UNHCR,IRC, Mercy Corps, or similar for pipeline and TA delivery
  • Measure and report on host-community vs. refugee client ratios to avoid social tension
Climate shock adaptations
  • Map portfolio to climate risk zones; flag clients in flood, drought, or cyclone-prone areas for enhanced monitoring
  • Offer automatic payment moratorium triggered by parametric climate event (index-based)
  • Bundle with agricultural index insurance or weather derivatives where available
  • Finance climate-adaptive inputs (drought-resistant seeds, solar irrigation, fuel-efficient cookstoves)
  • Prioritise women farmers. Evidence shows women reinvest more in household food security and climate adaptation
Economic crisis / macro shock adaptations
  • Activate portfolio-wide payment holiday review for women and excluded-group borrowers first
  • Adjust loan pricing to reflect currency devaluation impact on real repayment burden
  • Increase monitoring frequency from quarterly to monthly during macro shock period
  • Actively connect clients to government emergency grants or DFI crisis facilities. Do not treat these as competition
  • Review 2X eligibility criteria during crisis, businesses that slip below threshold temporarily should not be reclassified without contextual review
